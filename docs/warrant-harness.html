The RevealX Investigation Harness

An agentic harness that investigates RevealX detections end to end — resolving the entity, laddering through metrics, records, and packets, and handing back a verdict every conclusion of which is warranted by evidence it can show.

01

Design Principles

Six principles, each drawn from a practice the strongest investigation harnesses already prove out — reframed around what RevealX uniquely provides.

Principle 1
Entity-centric, not alert-centric

Consolidate every signal touching a device or peer group into one case, rather than triaging discrete alerts in isolation. RevealX already reasons in devices and roles; the harness inherits that spine.

Pattern: Corelight Agentic Triage, Conifers mesh.

Principle 2
Evidence-laddered depth

Escalate through metrics → records → packets only as far as the question demands. Cheap breadth first, byte-level proof last. This is the harness's core loop, and it is native to ExtraHop.

Pattern: L1→L2 investigation depth (D3, Dropzone).

Principle 3
Deterministic evidence, LLM reasoning

The model plans, hypothesizes, and narrates; ExtraHop's deterministic queries produce the facts. Conclusions are grounded in what was queried, not in model priors — which is what keeps hallucination out of a verdict.

Pattern: Intezer's dual-model, audit-ready evidence.

Principle 4
Glass-box by construction

Every query, pivot, and decision lands in an immutable case ledger. A reviewer can replay the whole chain from trigger to verdict. Defensibility is a property of the design, not a report bolted on at the end.

Pattern: D3 reasoning-chain criterion; rising regulatory bar.

Principle 5
Human-in-the-loop, confidence-gated

Autonomy is configurable per action class — read freely, escalate depth freely, but gate any write (tuning, containment) behind a confidence threshold and a human. Interruptible at any step.

Pattern: CAI HITL core; Torq autonomy modes.

Principle 6
Telemetry is untrusted input

Hostnames, URIs, user-agents, and certificate fields are attacker-controllable. They enter as data to be analyzed, never as instructions. Input guardrails sit between the wire and the model.

Pattern: CAI prompt-injection guardrails, adapted for defense.

03

The Evidence Ladder

The heart of the design. ExtraHop's own cost hierarchy — metrics are broad and cheap, records are deep, packets are proof — is exactly the discipline a good analyst uses. The harness makes it a rule.

Tier 1 Metrics Broad · cheap · always first

Where and when. Orient the case: bound the timeline, surface top talkers and anomalous volume, and generate the candidate blast radius. Most benign alerts should be closed here without ever touching a record.

Tier 2 Records Deep · bounded · on hypothesis

What happened. Once metrics point at a place and time, reconstruct the transaction: the specific connections, TLS handshakes, HTTP/DNS exchanges, and pivots between entities that confirm or kill the hypothesis. Always bounded by time window and filter.

04

The Investigation Loop

A detection enters; a warranted verdict leaves. Every step is logged to the case ledger.

Step 1
Trigger + Resolve

A detection fires. The Entity Resolver builds the case around the device or peer group, not the alert, and pulls related detections and threat-briefing context.

Step 2
Hypothesize

The orchestrator states an explicit hypothesis and its disconfirming test — what evidence would prove this benign — before any deep query runs.

Step 3
Climb the ladder

Metrics to orient, records to reconstruct, packets only if the deciding question demands proof. Each rung must justify the next.

Step 4
Map to ATT&CK

Confirmed activity is mapped to technique, so the verdict speaks the language the SOC already reports in.

Step 5
Verdict + confidence

A disposition with a confidence score and the full evidence chain attached — a conclusion, not another ticket.

Step 6
Gate + feedback

Below threshold or write-class action → human. Outcomes feed tuning proposals back — gated, never auto-applied — so noise shrinks over time.

05

Why This Design Wins

The claim is architectural, not benchmarked (see §06). On design merits, four things set it apart — and three of them are things only RevealX makes native.

1
Verdicts are packet-backed on one platform

The strongest commercial harnesses fight hallucination by bolting deterministic forensics onto LLM reasoning. RevealX already owns the ground truth: the same platform that raised the signal holds the records and the packets that prove it. The harness can climb from a behavioral anomaly all the way to the deciding byte without leaving the evidence source — audit-ready by construction, not by integration.

2
The evidence ladder is cost discipline and analyst-mimicry at once

Making metrics→records→packets a hard escalation rule means the harness spends cheap breadth on the many benign alerts and expensive depth only on the few that earn it. That is also exactly how a strong analyst works — so the reasoning chain reads as sound to the human reviewing it, not as a machine brute-forcing every query.

3
Glass-box meets the regulatory moment

As security decisions face growing legal and audit scrutiny, a verdict a reviewer can replay step by step is worth more than a faster black box. For regulated environments — healthcare, credit, public sector, critical infrastructure under MTSA and TSA directives — the immutable ledger is the difference between a finding that survives review and one that doesn't.

4
Detection-source awareness prevents the classic failure

The common mistake is trusting a signature match or an ML score as if it were a conclusion. By forcing corroboration for opaque ML detections and treating an IDS hit as a lead rather than a story, the harness refuses to close a case on a trigger alone — the single most reliable source of false verdicts in autonomous triage.

Contrast worth noting. Where an agentic layer is added on top of a detection engine after the fact, investigation tends to inherit the engine's alert-at-a-time shape and reaches for whatever evidence the layer was wired to. Building the harness around the evidence ladder from the start makes depth a first-class decision rather than a fixed lookup — and gives every verdict somewhere deeper to go when the answer isn't yet certain.

06

Areas for Improvement

Where this design is honestly still weak. Naming these is part of the argument — a harness whose limits are known is safer than one whose limits are discovered in production.

Cross-sensor entity resolution

Devices are local to a sensor's discovery context. An entity-centric case can fragment or double-count the same host seen by two sensors. The Entity Resolver needs a real federation/dedup layer, and this is non-trivial — get it wrong and blast-radius scoping is wrong.

ML detections stay partly opaque

Cloud ML scoring can be corroborated but not fully explained on-box. The glass-box is honest about metrics, records, and packets; for the ML trigger itself the ledger can show corroboration, not the original scoring logic.

Prompt injection from the wire

Adversary-controlled fields flow into model context. Principle 6 sets the intent, but robust input guardrails against injection embedded in hostnames, URIs, and certificate CNs are still an open engineering problem, not a solved one.

Packets may not be there

Tier 3 depends on a packetstore feed and retention window. For older activity the deciding bytes may be gone, or retrieval adds latency. Verdict confidence has to degrade gracefully when proof is unavailable, not stall.

No benchmark yet

"Best" here is a reasoned design claim. It needs an evaluation harness — labeled investigations, precision/recall on verdicts, and a tracked false-close rate — before the claim is defensible. This is the honest caveat on §05.

Air-gapped degradation

ML detections, threat intel, and briefings are cloud-fed. In air-gapped deployments the harness loses its accelerators and must lean harder on rule-based detections and records — a materially different, and weaker, operating mode.

Feedback loop can suppress real risk

Auto-tuning from verdicts is powerful and dangerous: a wrong verdict that writes a suppression hides a real threat. Tuning proposals must stay gated behind a human until verdict accuracy is independently trusted.

Analyst skill atrophy

A harness that closes most cases invites the team to stop looking. The HITL gates should be tuned to keep humans meaningfully engaged on the hard cases, not just rubber-stamping — a design responsibility, not only a config setting.
