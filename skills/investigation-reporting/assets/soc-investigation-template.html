Key Questions and Findings

Q1 — Is the TGS burst attacker tooling or an application misbehaving?

Attacker tooling. The pattern matches Kerberoasting, not application behavior.

Why it mattered: bulk RC4 ticket requests are the signature of Kerberoasting, but monitoring suites and legacy apps can look similar.

Observed23 TGS-REQs in 41s, every one explicitly requesting RC4-HMAC, against 19 distinct SPNs, from a host whose 30-day baseline is AES-only and ~2 TGS/hour (E1, E2).

ObservedThe burst was immediately preceded by first-seen LDAP SPN enumeration from the same host (E4).

AssessedThe enumerate-then-request sequence, uniform etype downgrade, and machine-speed pacing indicate credential-theft tooling (e.g. Rubeus/Impacket), not application behavior.

Confidence: High · T1558.003T1087.002
Q2 — Did the activity progress beyond credential access?

No use of the exposed credentials seen in the window — but offline cracking is invisible here.

Why it mattered: severity hinges on whether exposed hashes were cracked and used.

ObservedSMB reads of SYSVOL policy files from dc01 at 18:25Z (E3) — consistent with GPP/credential hunting, read-only.

ObservedNo authentication events by any of the 19 service accounts from any source through 21:30Z (E6); no admin-share access, no new-service installs, no anomalous outbound volume from the host (E7).

AssessedThe intrusion was caught in the credential-access stage; hashes are exposed for offline cracking but had not been used inside the monitored window.

Confidence: Moderate — offline cracking is invisible to network telemetry, and the window ends at 21:30Z · T1039
